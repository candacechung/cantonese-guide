🗣️ How to Read the Sounds (Yale Romanization)

Every syllable is an initial + a final + a tone. For example 好 = hóu → h (initial) + ou (final) + the rising-tone mark ´. Yale shows the tone two ways at once: an accent mark over the vowel for the pitch, and a silent “h” after the vowel to mark a low tone.

The 6 Cantonese Tones in Yale
ToneNameHow Yale shows itExample
1Highmacron ā (or high-falling à)詩 sī
2Mid-risingacute accent á史 sí
3Mid-levelno mark試 si
4Low-fallinggrave accent + h → àh時 sìh
5Low-risingacute accent + h → áh市 síh
6Low-levelh only是 sih

For reference, here is how Yale compares with Jyutping (another common system). This course always uses Yale — the comparison is only so you recognise the difference if you meet Jyutping elsewhere.

📝 Grammar Playground

Learn how to say "Yes," "No," "Good," and "Bad"!

The "Is" vs "Is Not"

To Be / Yes 係 haih

Use this to say what something IS.

Not To Be / No 唔係 m̀h haih

Use this to say what something IS NOT.

The "Good" vs "Not Good"

Good / Well 好 hóu

Use this for "Good" or "Very."

Not Good / Bad 唔好 m̀h hóu

Use this for "Not Good" or "Don't."

🚀 Sentence Builder Challenge:

Combine these with your food words!

  • ✅ 好食 (hóu sihk) = Good to eat (Delicious!)
  • ❌ 唔好食 (m̀h hóu sihk) = Not good to eat (Yucky!)
  • ✅ 係魚 (haih yú) = It is a fish.
  • ❌ 唔係魚 (m̀h haih yú) = It is not a fish.

The "Have" vs "Not Have" (Existence)

To Have / There is 有 yáuh

Use this for things you OWN or that EXIST.

To Not Have / There is not 無 mòuh

Use this when something is MISSING.

💡 Pro Tip: Existence Questions

Just like "好唔好", you can combine these to ask "Do you have?":

「有無...?」 (yáuh mòuh...?) = "Have or not have...?"

  • ✅ 我有魚 (ngóh yáuh yú) = I have fish.
  • ❌ 我無錢 (ngóh mòuh chín) = I have no money.
  • ❓ 有無叉燒包? (yáuh mòuh chā sīu bāau?) = Do you have BBQ pork buns?

⏳ Verb Time: Tenses & Aspect

Good news — Cantonese verbs never change form! You add one small word to show when.

🔮 Future — 會 (will)

Put 會 (wúih) before the verb to say you will do something.

我會去ngóh wúih heui
I will go
我會食飯ngóh wúih sihk faahn
I will eat
聽日會落雨tīng yaht wúih lohk yúh
It will rain tomorrow

✅ Past / Done — 咗 (completed)

Add 咗 (jó) right after the verb to show the action is finished.

我去咗ngóh heui jó
I went / have gone
我食咗飯ngóh sihk jó faahn
I have eaten
佢返咗工kéuih fāan jó gūng
He/She went to work

⏱️ Right Now — 緊 (-ing)

Add 緊 (gán) after the verb for an action happening now.

我食緊飯ngóh sihk gán faahn
I am eating
我行緊山ngóh hàahng gán sāan
I am hiking

🧳 Ever Done — 過 (experience)

Add 過 (gwo) after the verb to say you have ever done it before.

我去過ngóh heui gwo
I have been there before
我食過ngóh sihk gwo
I have eaten it before

💡 Quick Recap

  • 🔮 會 (wúih) before the verb = will (future): 我會去
  • ✅ 咗 (jó) after the verb = done (past): 我去咗
  • ⏱️ 緊 (gán) after the verb = -ing (now): 我食緊飯
  • 🧳 過 (gwo) after the verb = ever (experience): 我去過

💡 Pro Tip: The "Fāan" (返) Rule

In Cantonese, we don't just "go" to work or home. We use the word 返 (fāan), which means "to return." Whether it's your office or your house, you are "returning" to where you belong!

Example: ngóh fāan ūk kéi = I am going (returning) home.
